# The 3P Framework

Most people who struggle with health consistency aren't missing willpower. They're missing one piece of a three-part system. I call this the 3P framework.

Three pieces. Each one does work the other two can't.

---

### Proof. Knowing what actually works.

Proof is basic health literacy. The fundamentals of how your body works and what reliably keeps it well. Not specialist knowledge. Not advanced protocols. The foundational layer that most adults were never properly taught.

The industry is always finding the next thing. Cold plunges. Red light therapy. Continuous glucose monitors. Personalized supplementation.

But decades of research already point to the same six fundamentals: nutrition, movement, sleep, stress management, social connection, avoiding harmful substances. Settled science. Not as sellable as whatever launched last month. It's what actually works.

Proof also means protecting yourself from health misinformation and pseudoscience. The wellness industry profits from making things complicated. The evidence base helps you separate what works from what's been packaged to look like it does. Once you have the basics, the noise stops being convincing.

Biohacking the edges while neglecting the fundamentals is like optimizing your sprinkler system while your house is on fire.

Almost every newsletter, podcast, and Instagram post you've read about health lives in the Proof layer. That's the part the industry is happy to talk about. The other two are where it goes silent.

---

### Practice. A system that bends with real life.

Knowing what to do is not the same as doing it on a deadline Wednesday with three meetings and no time to think.

Practice is a set of skills. It lets you build your own system, one that holds whether life is peaceful or chaotic. You learn how to adjust the intensity of a habit instead of dropping it. You learn how to come back without making a setback bigger than it needs to be. You learn what a habit looks like at full effort and what it looks like at the smallest version that still counts.

The skills give you something to fall back on when the plan stops working. Which is when most people quit. Practice is what lets you stay in motion through the hard weeks instead of restarting after them.

Most health guidance stops exactly where real life starts. Practice begins there.

---

### Purpose. A reason that outlasts the latest goal.

Most motivation runs on fear. Fear of disease. Fear of decline. Fear of the number on the scale or the lab result.

Fear is effective as a starting point. It doesn't carry you through the Wednesday when everything falls apart. Research shows only 4.3% of people sustain behavior change after a health scare, the most visceral motivator imaginable. If a heart attack can't do it, a before-and-after photo definitely can't.

Purpose is what runs deeper than fear. Usually connected to someone you love, a life you're building, a version of yourself you've decided to become. Something that's still true on the worst Wednesday of the year.

**How the pieces work together**

One piece isn't enough. Two pieces hold for a while, then break in predictable ways.

Proof alone gets stuck in research mode. You know what to do and you don't do it.

Practice alone runs on inertia. You build a routine and it works until life disrupts it, with no foundation underneath to rebuild from.

Purpose alone burns through motivation. You care deeply and have no idea where to channel it, so the energy dissipates.

All three together is what people are actually pointing at when they say "I just need to be consistent." Consistency is the result. The three pieces are the cause.
